So you’re interested in coming to Burning Man? Great choice! We have found that the people who tend to have the most fun are also the most prepared. Part of the fun is that the environment can be very challenging. Despite your best efforts, there will almost always be parts that will be difficult and disheartening, but, also, very rewarding. As you will soon see, it is definitely a rabbit hole. Every time you make a decision that you think will make things easier, it just opens up more decision/responsibility trees! This guide is meant to aid you in your journey, in rough order of importance. In terms of the logistics, here are the details that must be worked out:

1.    Tickets

a.    Are you obtaining them through our camp or elsewhere? Historically they have been difficult to come by, but beginning in 2023 this has changed and they have been a bit easier since, often with a large surplus near the event with people selling them below face value. As a camp, we discourage people from trying to get cheaper tickets in this way, as you are basically just having another burner subsidize your ticket, and is as much against the spirit of the event as scalping tickets (highly discouraged/forbidden) when they were difficult to come by. Ask whomever is sponsoring you at Camp Fly if there are camp tickets available or if you will need to obtain them some other way. Information on tickets, and when they will go on sale can be found at https://burningman.org/black-rock-city/ticketing-information/

2.    Getting to and from the event, there are several options:

a.    Driving yourself there and back, fairly self explanatory, you will need a vehicle pass to get in and out. You are allowed as many people in each vehicle as can fit, they each need their own event ticket but one vehicle pass covers them all. The vehicle pass only grants you the ability to drive to and from camp once, not to drive around the event.

b.    Get a ride from a campmate, coordinate with a camp lead (Grant, Chris or Doug), or if you are adventurous, an online burner forum

c.     Fly to Reno, then:

                                               i.     Fly in to the event on a small puddle jumper. Obviously the most expensive/baller option. About 30 minutes. Limited flight times available, book in advance (flights also available from san Francisco)

                                             ii.     Take the burner express bus. This option cuts the line at gate and brings you from the reno airport right to the playa. Limited times available and they sell out quickly

                                            iii.     Rent a car at reno airport and drive to the playa- expensive, have to clean the car really well, but works well especially if you have several people on the same flight as it allows you to go buy water etc on the way. You will need a vehicle pass of course.

3.    Lodging- there are several options for how to shelter yourself, discussed below in greater detail:

a.    Tent- the cheapest option will be a tent- it is also the dustiest and hottest method. Would generally recommend something as large as possible.

b.    Car/cargo van- simply outfitting a car, truck or van so that you can sleep in it comfortably. This is a very popular option and can work well if you have a vehicle already this will work for, or can be a cheaper option if you are renting rather than renting an RV. Some work involved in making it livable but it works and is a pretty common option.

c.     There is a very popular option made by a burner called a shiftpod. These are large enough for a queen size bed, a wardrobe, and chair. They are insulated, light reflecting, and have vents for you to add a swamp cooler or air conditioner. They are very easy to set up, but are heavy and fairly bulky. There are three options for cooling your shiftpod:

                                               i.     doing nothing- could maybe use a battery operated fan or something, if we get a cool weather year this could be fine.

                                             ii.     Swamp cooler- generally homemade but I have seen them online. Here is a popular formula that many burners use. These don’t take much power and can run off DC power like a car battery, but you will need to recharge the battery periodically using a solar panel or some other method and you will need to add water as well. An Achilles heel of this style is that the little DC water pump will burn out if you run out of water, so I would recommend bringing extra if you go this route.

                                            iii.     Portable air conditioner. Biggest issue with these is that you will also need to buy a large enough generator to run the AC unit. Test your extension cord, generator, and AC unit extensively before lugging them all to the playa to make sure they all work together.

d.    RV’s- clearly the most baller route, but once this decision is made, it is a good example of burning man rabbit holes as it really is just the beginning of what you need to coordinate. There is a lot here, so I will break this down into categories.

                                               i.     Do you own, rent, or are you buying an RV?

1.    Own- So you already own an RV, great! Some questions to ask yourself- have you used it a lot? Do you know where all the bodies are buried/aka fix things when they break/aren’t working as expected? Do you have solar? An on-board generator? Do you know what size tanks you have? If you have used your RV a lot already and are comfortable with “boondocking” aka camping without hookups, that will help tremendously. If not, read on for further information regarding the various options and systems.

2.    Rent- Ok, so you are going to rent an RV. Bear in mind that 70,000 people go to burning man every year, many of them in rental RV’s. Being that Labor Day is the last day of the event, it is already a popular week for renting an RV. Add to that, many people do not wish their RV’s to be used for burning man, so it can get tricky/expensive to find a willing owner. There are sites, outdoorsy.com being the most well known, that allow you to search for “festival friendly” rentals. Do yourself and the community a favor and don’t lie about the use, it gives burning man a bad name, and trust me, there is no hiding the fact that you went to the burn. Picking up in cities near the event such as Reno, northern California or even AZ can be 2x the price due to the demand surge for the event. I have seen the best prices in Denver and Salt Lake City in terms of proximity to the event. Please also be aware that this will be the end of the rental season so the chances that the rental company will be delivering you a rental with issues they are already aware of but had not time to fix because it is rented back to back- is high. They of course pretend to not know, then ship the rental on to the next person. You should read on about the major systems in RV’s in order to get a handle on what you should be looking for. I would say about half the time, rental RV’s show up with some critical system not working- fridge, AC, generator, etc.

3.    Buy. If you can swing it, even if you go to sell it the next year and have time to mess with all of that, this can actually compete as an option with renting in terms of expense, if you buy and sell well.

                                             ii.     Styles of RV’s

1.    Towable- just like it sounds like, these are RV’s you tow behind a vehicle. An advantage to these is that you don’t need to purchase an automobile style insurance policy as the insurance follows the tow vehicle. A very important consideration with this style is the capability of your tow vehicle- aka, can you tow this much weight. Most trailers start around 4k lbs and ideally you should have a little extra. They do make small, lightweight teardrop style trailers but at that point, they are little more than tents- no bathroom, no ac, outdoor kitchen. At that point, personally, I would rather have a shiftpod as they provide a lot more living space.

2.    Drivable- several styles- Class A (bus/flat front), Class B (Mercedes sprinter type), Class C (truck/van front). These come in all different sizes, shapes, engine types.

                                            iii.     Major systems of RV’s you should be aware of

1.    Water and waste tanks- you should be aware of the size of your tanks, and it would be great if you had an idea (from using it previously) how much water you will use and put into your tanks. There are many different ways tank sensors work and it is a good idea to familiarize yourself with what your tank lights/sensor lights mean and what their limitations are. For instance a common style will be a series of three lights so being at 2/3 full will show you the same as being just over 1/3 full. Just good to know how yours works. Also, make sure you test your waste tanks, even on the way out if you have to, to identify leaks that may be present. If your grey water or black water tank are leaking, you may not be able to use them at the burn depending on severity. Better to identify on the way when you are within reach of a home depot.

a.    Fresh water tank- this is the clean water you bring to drink, cook with, shower and use your toilet with. Some people opt to augment their supply by bringing separate drinking water. I recommend flexible 5 gallon collapsible water jugs for this as they are lightweight and can collapse down when you are done with them. The hard plastic ones like to fly away when they are empty. You can also just buy 1-2 gallon jugs of water for drinking.

b.    Grey water tank- this is where your shower and kitchen sink drain into. They fill up quickly unless you are very careful. Especially with dishes- can be sneaky- oftentimes people are used to running the tap when cleaning up, so I recommend bringing a spray bottle to clean dishes and wet your sponge with rather than running the faucet to do those things. We also use paper plates mostly to cut down on dishes and think out our menu plan so we are doing as few dishes as possible.

c.     Black water tank- this is where your toilet flushes into, and depending on your RV, your bathroom sink may also drain into this as well.

2.    Air conditioning

a.    Bear in mind that air conditioning takes a lot of power. If your RV has an on board generator, you will need to run it in order to run your air conditioner. Make sure you bring enough gas to get you to and from the playa, plus run your generator. If you are renting, make sure you understand their policy regarding how much generator use is allowed and what the overage costs are. If your RV does not have an on board generator, then you will need to purchase and bring it with you, and bring the gas you will need to run it.

3.    Electrical system- make sure you understand the electrical system of your RV.

a.    How many amp hours does it have?

b.    What types of batteries, e.g. lead acid/AGM/Lithium?

                                                                                                     i.     Do you have an inverter for running AC type appliances off your battery bank?

                                                                                                   ii.     How will you recharge your house batteries?

                                                                                                  iii.     Do you have solar capacity and if so how many panels and what is designed to run off the solar? Oftentimes solar panels and your house battery bank will not provide you enough power to run your air conditioner unless you invested heavily into this capability.

                                                                                                  iv.     Do you know where the main shut offs and circuit breakers are and what they control?

4.    Slides- does your RV have slides? If so, do you know how to manually override them in case of issues?

4.    Food- this is highly personal and will depend on what lodging you are bringing, but here are some thoughts and tips. Generally speaking, a little forethought- making sure you have the food you will be happy eating, goes a long way towards your on playa happiness.

a.    There is a lot of food being given away on playa. If you aren’t choosy and don’t mind lines, this is a good way to go and meet people and take advantage of what the playa will provide

b.    Backpacker meals are the easiest, cleanest, lightest, least overhead way of feeding yourself, say if you are camping in a small tent, flying in, etc. It works.

c.     If you are bringing a cooler and or fridge in an RV, an easy option can be to go around to your favorite carry out restaurants and just buy food and freeze it. Things in sauce work well- think Indian and thai currys. Meals like Kevin’s paleo works well.

d.    In the months leading up to the burn, if you have leftovers, consider freezing some for the burn and labeling them well. Saves some brain damage as you get closer. Frozen meals serve as ice packs in a cooler for the first few days.

e.    Sous vide meat works well and is very food safe.

f.      Consider vegetables and sides that don’t need refrigeration- mac and cheese, potatoes, instant mashed potatoes, etc.

g.    Camp Fly provides a large grill and burner for communal use so that you can cook outside, not smell/heat up your RV or tent.

h.    Consider bringing a cooler. They are more bomb proof than RV fridges, as they will always work if you just add ice. Ice is the only item that can be purchased at burning man. All other sales of everything are prohibited.

i.      Dry ice (available from most grocery stores at the customer service desk) can drop the temp of your cooler and food down by another 10-20 degrees and will sublimate (evaporate) rather than turn to water so less mess. This can give you an extra 2-4 days of cooling power to start the week off, where you don’t need to add ice. This can give you the ability to fill your cooler completely, and then make room for some ice as you eat.

j.      Don’t bring meals that will require a lot of prep and create dishes or trash. We use paper plates to cut down on water use in the RV and live with the extra trash, but all vegetables arrive to the playa prepped, all meals cooked and just require grilling or reheating. Saves time, hassle, mess, and trash.

k.     Biased because it is one of my favorite foods, but breakfast burritos make a nice, filling breakfast and are easy to clean up and reheat, and can be pretty easily purchased if you don’t feel like cooking your own.

l.      Water/hydration- remember that you are going to be in a desert. One of the more common mistakes we see people make is not properly hydrating. It can take a day or two to recover fully from allowing yourself to get dehydrated.

Bring Nalgene or hydroflask wide mouth water bottles, and bring them with you everywhere. Important to drink a shit load of water, remember it’s a desert 1-2 gallons per day. Bring self adhesive labels and you can write your name and camp address on them. Highly doubt someone will return them but you never know. You will need a bunch, think four each. Each night you will go out with two each filled with water and 1-2 with some kind of alcohol. One more to have around camp.

    11. Some not to do’s as far as clothing:

                                               i.     Don’t wear any clothing with logos, one of the tenets of Burning Man is de-commoditization- no advertising allowed at all especially on clothing. 

                                             ii.     No feather Boa’s, they literally screen for these at the gate even though they don’t care about practically anything else (the burning man police anyway)- they cause litter- otherwise known as MOOP at burning man (Matter Out Of Place). Another tenet- leave no trace, pick up all MOOP you see and definitely don’t leave it out there yourself. Accept this rule generally- don’t buy any clothing or other camp gear that will MOOP. Yes it is both an adjective and a verb 

                                            iii.     Don’t wear anything you would feel comfortable going to the grocery store in. Another Tenet- everyone participates/dresses up/is a part of the whole scene 

                                            iv.     Don’t plan on wearing uncomfortable shoes. I heard once that “outfits stop at the ankle” but honestly, most people wore shoes that looked cool. My first year my boots hurt so bad that I ended up wearing some slip on black shoes I got at the last second at a Walmart en route basically every day. They kind of saved me. Make sure you have something comfortable. Walmart is a good place for this. Last year, my go to boots were these. The first year I went for some kind of super goth boot like this, and they just hurt too much to be in. Wore them one night. You’re basically going to be standing/walking all night so it’s important your shoes are helping that situation… Also good to have camp shoes. Don’t plan on flip flops or barefeet, the playa dust is super alkaline and can really mess up your feet. I personally don’t have issues with this but it really messes some people up, if you are the brave sort, google “playa feet”.  

  1. On Playa transportation: Your vehicle pass you may or may not have purchased only gives you the right to drive your car, once, in from gate road into camp, and out from camp back home, that’s it. You will want something else, most often a Cruiser bike. Absolute must have, it is the fastest way to get around (even faster than art cars because the 5 mph speed limit is strictly enforced for art cars, no speed limit on a bike). If you have a cruiser bike already and don’t mind cleaning the shit out of it when you get back, cool. Bring dry chain lube, with the dust your chain will be crying in short order. If you have a one wheel or e bike, those are popular as well. Make sure you think through about how to get the bike to and from the playa. Camp Fly has a limited number of spares available, talk to a camp lead (Grant, Chris, or Doug) to coordinate and see if one may be available.
  2. Familiarize yourself with the burning man principles. They will aid in your understanding of the event, connect with the ethos, and help prepare you for the event. The original org text is included with why I feel they matter below.
    1. Radical Inclusion: Anyone may be a part of Burning Man; no prerequisites exist for participation, and the community welcomes and respects the stranger.

                                               i.     Don’t be a douche bag. This is the friendliest group of humans I have ever met but there are exceptions of course and those people suck. Don’t be those guys.

    1. Gifting: The community is devoted to acts of unconditional gift-giving that do not contemplate a return or an exchange.

                                               i.     There is not buying or bartering. Just give things away to be cool. Our camp wide gift, which you are taking part of by being part of our camp, is the art cars. Bringing them, sharing them, giving people rides, etc. That is our camp gift and anyone camping with us is “doing it right” as far as gifting simply by supporting us. But of course you can always take this further based on your own personal interests. Do a juggling show, make jewlery, whatever. In general think of the first year as mostly worrying about your own logistics as there are a lot, and support the camp. For future years, you will have your feet under you and will have more ideas about what might constitute good gifts should you feel compelled to do more.

    1. Decommodification: To preserve the spirit of gifting, the environment is unmediated by commercial sponsorships, transactions, or advertising.

                                               i.     Kind of a cool break to not have advertisements. Ideally, we should be covering up the brand names on our RV’s, etc, we could be better about this as a camp, but certainly not a good time to wear clothing with brand names on them.

    1. Radical Self-reliance: Individuals are encouraged to discover and rely on their own inner resources to survive in the harsh desert environment.

                                               i.     This one is critical to your own enjoyment, and frankly to how the rest of the people at the camp and the burn experiences your presence at the event as well. It is possible to show up at the event totally unprepared and survive off the largesse of others, and some people do that. But I would wager those people have less fun, and result in other people having less fun. No one does this perfect but effort matters. It is ok to let someone lend you a tool or fix something for you, of course- it is more just trying to have your logistics figured out that matters. If you aren’t taking care of all this, say you are staying in someones RV, then think through how you can support them- maybe you do all the cooking and/or dishes. That is still being self-reliant because you are communally agreeing to take care of each other and relying on each other. But showing up and not reciprocating any help you receive through effort is not the right idea.  

    1. Radical Self-expression: This arises from the unique gifts of the individual and is offered as a gift to others, while respecting the rights of the recipient.

                                               i.     Fly your freak flag, bring your art, stoke people out.

    1. Communal Effort: The community values creative cooperation and collaboration to produce and protect social networks and works of art.

                                               i.     Help the camp out, help other burners out, it’s fun

    1. Civic Responsibility: Community members assume responsibility for public welfare and must conduct events in accordance with local, state, and federal laws.

                                               i.     No attendees or volunteers at burning man want to be authority figures. Try and get through whatever you need to by just getting along.

    1. Leaving No Trace: Participants are committed to leaving no physical trace of their activities and endeavor to leave places in a better state than they found them.

                                               i.     Plan your event around not bringing and leaving trash on the playa. Pick up MOOP (Matter Out Of Place), basically anything other than playa dust, that you see, and take it home, all the way home with you, with the rest of your trash. There are no public trash cans at burning man, only what we bring with us.

    1. Participation: The community is committed to a radically participatory ethic, believing that transformative change occurs through deeply personal doing.

                                               i.     There are no spectators. What are you doing to delight and amuse? The answer is already, you are helping bring the art cars to the playa. By camping with us you are taking part in this, but by all means, add more. There should not be people who just show up, looking solely to be amused by others, there should be a give and take. The person juggling in front of 500 people one day is getting a read a book during topless Asian story hour (a thing I have seen) the next day.

    1. Immediacy: This is a primary touchstone of value, seeking to overcome barriers between individuals and their inner selves and the reality of those around them.

                                               i.     Put your phone down for a week, try not to plan too far ahead, and try to say yes when people try and talk you into things.

  1. Camp Dues/Roles/Contributions- Camp Fly is an intimate group, very small for a burning man camp, and is invite only. We do not collect camp dues. Camp dues normally range from a couple hundred to a couple thousand dollars. This is a common way larger camps raise money to offset the cost of their offering. For instance, a large sound camp costs millions of dollars to set up and literally will have year round employed staff to bring the thing to fruition. They might have a tent city where their volunteers stay, and charge $1500 per person, but provide food, an open bar, showers, a huge generator/power grid, mandatory work shifts etc. Camp Fly, for better and worse, offers our campers nothing other than a communal shade structure with seating and a grill. No shared workshifts, no mandatory anything. But, the reason our camp exists/receives allotments of space and tickets from org is because we bring out art cars which represent a large investment of time, money, and energy. Camping with camp fly piggy backs you on to that effort, so a good question to ask yourself is, if you don’t own an art car, then how are you supporting the people who do, to make their lives more fun or easy, before, during, or after the event? This can be cooking a meal, doing more of the camp cleaning, really whatever you want. We all appreciate being recognized for what we do for the camp through reciprocal acts of service, and rather than charge dues, we would prefer to keep it loose and in a spirit of gratitude. If you read all this and think to yourself, “cool, I am saving on camp dues”, you might not get invited back, and also, from experience, those are the types of people who I see have less fun at the event. They just don’t connect with the beauty of the overarching vibe of the event. If you aren’t sure of what to do or how you would like to help reach out to a camp lead (Grant, Chris, or Doug) for suggestions.
